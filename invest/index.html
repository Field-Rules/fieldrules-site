Investment Thesis

Your best people know things
your AI never will — unless
someone writes them down.

FieldRules is a system for capturing why teams do things — the judgment, exceptions, and reasoning that AI can't scrape, infer, or generate. We turn tacit expertise into structured rules that govern how AI behaves.

Pre-Seed · B2B SaaS · Two founders, pre-revenue, obsessively deep
01 — How We Got Here

We built this because we watched the problem happen

This started from the operator seat. Watching customer success teams scale, the pattern was always the same: the best people carried the real playbook in their heads. Not in Confluence. Not in the wiki. In their judgment.

Then AI showed up. Copilots drafted responses. Agents triaged tickets. Workflows got faster. And something quietly broke: the AI consumed the what — what to do, what to say, what to escalate — but it had zero access to the why.

Senior reps left. New reps inherited AI-generated playbooks with no provenance. The question "why do we do it this way?" became unanswerable. Not because nobody knew — because nobody had ever written it down in a format that survived their departure.

Three things are true simultaneously, and the intersection is the company:

1. AI is accelerating execution. LLMs, copilots, and agent frameworks are compressing the time from intent to action. This is real and irreversible.

2. The reasoning is leaking. The experts who know why a policy exists, why an exception matters, why a renewal playbook deviates from the template — that knowledge isn't in any system.

3. Every AI infrastructure tool assumes governance already exists. LangChain, Guardrails AI, HumanLayer — they build harnesses for how AI executes. None of them capture who authored the rules or why.

02 — The Difference

This is documentation. This is reasoning.

The left side is what documentation produces. The right side is what FieldRules elicits — because it asks "what would go wrong if this rule didn't exist?" instead of "why does this rule exist?"

That single design choice is the difference between compliance and reasoning.

What Documentation Produces
IF a trial site submits enrollment within 14 days with <3 coordinators THEN assign specialist & schedule check-in
Because "Because it's important to catch these early."
What FieldRules Elicits
IF a trial site submits enrollment within 14 days with <3 coordinators THEN assign specialist & schedule check-in
Because "Sites this size with fast enrollment timelines are statistically high-dropout in weeks 3–5. Early intervention cuts churn by over half — but only if flagged before the standard onboarding flow locks in."
The first is what you get when you ask someone to document. The second is what you get when you ask "what would go wrong if this rule didn't exist?" — a contrastive prompt that forces experts to name consequences rather than narrate intent. Consequence-naming exposes tautology and surfaces the causal chain that makes a rule enforceable. FieldRules doesn't ask experts to explain. It asks them to defend.
03 — What We Build

Every rule has three fields.
The third one is the product.

The first two fields — IF and THEN — can be AI-drafted from a support ticket in seconds. The third — BECAUSE — is always blank until a domain expert writes it. That's the entire product thesis in one design decision.

Clinical Operations SaaS · operational playbook Identified from ticket #4112
IF
A trial site submits their first patient enrollment report within 14 days of activation AND their coordinator headcount is below 3
THEN
Do not auto-advance to full onboarding. Assign a dedicated specialist and schedule a 48-hour check-in before the site goes live.
BECAUSE
Sites this size with fast enrollment timelines are statistically high-dropout in weeks 3–5. Early intervention cuts churn by over half — but only if flagged before the standard onboarding flow locks in.
Only humans write this field
J
Jordan · CS Lead · 45 sec in Slack
Confirmed

Jordan doesn't open a dashboard. She confirms a Slack card from a ticket she already handled. 45 seconds. The BECAUSE field is the one thing she writes — in her own words, right when the context is freshest.

The rule is versioned, attributed, and queryable. When a PM's query hits a gap, the domain expert gets a live signal: "Alex needs your expertise on [topic] before sending to engineering." When an AI agent reads this rule, it acts within a governed boundary — not from training data defaults.

Every knowledge tool in the market treats contribution as binary — document exists or it doesn't. Nobody measures the quality of what went in. The Reasoning Health Score is a category-defining metric: it answers "are your team's captured rules actually reasoning, or are they filling in forms?"

It detects six anti-patterns in real time — rubber stamping, tautological reasoning, copy-paste drift, signal gaming, declining depth, and speed-through — so the library gets measurably stronger over time, not just bigger.

04 — Why Now

This problem is decades old. Three things just changed.

Enterprises are actually deploying agents. Not experimenting — deploying. The Bain Technology Report 2025 argues that agentic AI governance must be designed "in lockstep with architecture" — not bolted on afterward. Companies that gave copilots a pass on governance are discovering that agents — which take actions autonomously — cannot run without it.

LLMs made the elicitation UX possible. Every prior knowledge engineering attempt died on the authoring burden — expert systems in the '80s, knowledge graphs in the '10s. Now the AI drafts the rule; the human authors the reasoning. That division of labor didn't exist two years ago.

The smartest people in AI are naming this exact gap. Andrej Karpathy identifies the bottleneck for AI capability as the absence of "inner thought monologues" — the reasoning traces of expert problem-solving. Terence Tao calls AI outputs "odorless proofs" — formally correct but missing the reasoning penumbra that makes them useful. The BECAUSE field is that monologue. It's that penumbra. Both of them are describing the data type FieldRules captures.

The elicitation lab runs continuously — an AI-powered research loop that generates UX variants from cognitive science literature, tests each against synthetic personas representing different expertise levels and cognitive styles, then feeds findings back into the next cycle. One finding from our adversarial pass: persona Priya demonstrated that starter chips with a low quality threshold enabled 15-second boilerplate submissions. We caught it before any user saw it, redesigned the quality gate, and re-ran the eval loop.

We have a seven-layer elicitation loop, a four-tier response system (prompt fix → few-shot → embedding → fine-tune), a seed library generator that researches prospect companies via web search, and a reasoning advantage eval. In internal, blind contrastive testing against sample Confluence docs and call transcripts, human-authored BECAUSE fields outscored model-generated reasoning on our quality rubric. This is synthetic pre-pilot evidence, not customer data — full methodology and N ship with pilot results.

We built a strategy ladder with four named bets (Governance, Practitioner, Timing, Data Layer), each with kill conditions. We built an H3 framework (Helpful, Honest, Harmless) that governs every AI interaction in the product. We built an anti-hallucination credibility plan that gates external claims behind pilot evidence.

This is what happens when the founders care more about being right than being first.

We're building the governance layer that sits underneath CS platforms — and underneath every domain where teams make judgment calls that AI is about to inherit. We don't have a five-year roadmap pretending we know what happens in year four. We have a sequence of gates:

Gate 1 — This is the raise

Practitioners author high-quality BECAUSE fields

If CS practitioners consistently produce non-tautological reasoning — judgment, not restatement — the core value loop works. The entire elicitation research lab exists to force this gate open. If we can't clear it, the rest doesn't matter.

Gate 2

Governed AI surfaces outperform ungoverned ones

If AI reading FieldRules-governed rules produces measurably better outcomes — fewer escalations, higher renewal rates, faster ramp — the ROI case writes itself. We have the eval infrastructure to prove this from pilot day one.

Gate 3

The library compounds into a skill graph

Rules reference other rules. Every new rule makes every existing rule more valuable. At this point FieldRules is generating a traversable knowledge graph that didn't exist before we created the surface for it. This is the data moat — it can't be exported without losing all relational structure.

Gate 4

The structure ports beyond CS

IF/THEN/BECAUSE works anywhere experts carry tacit operational judgment. If the authoring UX ports to a second domain, the TAM expands by an order of magnitude.
